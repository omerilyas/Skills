# Audience Prompts

Reusable phrasings of the seven intake questions the skill must ask before drafting any slide. Pick the variant that fits the user's tone and how much they've already shared. Don't skip questions — but you can compress two questions into one if the user has already partially answered.

The seven intake questions are:

1. Audience
2. Purpose
3. Time available
4. Technical depth
5. Desired outcome
6. Presentation style
7. Existing material

Below: 3-5 phrasings each, plus follow-ups for vague answers.

---

## 1. Audience

### Direct

> Who is in the room? Titles, level of seniority, and how technical they are.

### Conversational

> Tell me about the audience. Who exactly is sitting there, and what do they care about coming in?

### When the user already named a title

> You said "[their answer]". Quick clarifications:
> - How senior are we talking? (e.g. VP, SVP, board?)
> - Internal or external?
> - Anyone else likely to be in the room — staff, peers, decision blockers?

### Follow-up when vague

> "Executives" can mean anything from a director who codes on weekends to a board member who has never opened your product. Can you narrow it down?

### Follow-up when they say "mixed"

> Mixed audiences are hard. Who is the *primary* audience — the person whose decision matters most? We'll calibrate to them and provide for the others.

---

## 2. Purpose

### Direct

> Why does this presentation exist? What outcome, decision, or action are you trying to drive?

### Conversational

> What's the job this presentation is doing? If it goes well, what changes after you walk out of that room?

### When the user said "to inform"

> "To inform" is rarely the real reason — it usually hides a real ask. Are you trying to get a decision, secure budget, align a team, set up a follow-up, or something else?

### Follow-up when vague

> Sharpen this for me. If the audience walked out and did *nothing differently*, would the presentation have been a waste of time? What's the smallest concrete change you want?

---

## 3. Time available

### Direct

> How long is the slot? Is there Q&A time built in, or is the whole slot for presenting?

### Conversational

> How much time do you have? Hard stop or flexible? And how much of that do you expect to actually be presenting versus discussing?

### Follow-up when "30 minutes"

> 30 minutes total or 30 minutes plus Q&A? In my experience the real presenting time is half what's on the calendar.

### Follow-up when "as long as I need"

> Open-ended slots are dangerous — they almost always run long and lose the audience. What's the realistic upper bound? What's your target?

---

## 4. Technical depth

### Direct

> How technical can we go? Where is this audience on the spectrum from "no jargon at all" to "engineering deep dive"?

### Conversational

> What level of technical detail does this audience tolerate — and want? Any specific terms I should avoid or specifically use?

### Follow-up when "they're technical"

> Technical in what way? Architects can go deep on systems but get lost on ML; data folks are the opposite. What's their actual area?

### Follow-up when audience has mixed depth

> If half the room is technical and half isn't, you have to pick a level. The technical half can tolerate slightly less depth more than the non-technical half can tolerate slightly more. Lean toward the less-technical half. Confirm?

---

## 5. Desired outcome

### Direct

> What does success look like at the end? What specifically do you want to walk out with?

### Conversational

> Imagine the meeting ends well — what just happened? A signed-off decision? Aligned team? Booked follow-up? Be concrete.

### Follow-up when "I want them to like it"

> Liking it isn't an outcome — it's a side effect. What action do you want from them? An approval, a yes, a follow-up, a referral, an introduction?

### Follow-up to surface fallback asks

> Best case is what you said. What's the *acceptable* outcome — the smaller thing that's still a win? Most pitches benefit from a primary ask and a fallback.

---

## 6. Presentation style

### Direct

> Formal exec briefing, conversational customer pitch, whiteboard-style technical session, demo-led story, or hands-on workshop?

### Conversational

> What's the energy you want? Polished and tight, or conversational and discovery-shaped? Demo-led or slides-led? Lecture or interactive?

### Follow-up when unsure

> Default to the audience's preference. Execs usually prefer formal-but-tight. Engineers usually prefer whiteboard-and-questions. Customers usually prefer conversational. Which fits?

---

## 7. Existing material

### Direct

> What do you already have? Notes, transcripts, prior decks, architecture docs, whitepapers, demos, recordings — list everything.

### Conversational

> Walk me through what you've already got. The messier the better — I'd rather see raw thinking than a half-polished draft.

### Follow-up to surface hidden material

> Anything you wrote on the back of a napkin or in a Slack thread? Customer asks captured in email? Prior conversations on this same topic? Often the best raw material isn't the formal documents.

### Follow-up on data / metrics

> You mentioned [metric / number / claim]. Is that validated, or is it your estimate? If estimate, who would I cite if challenged? Audiences press on numbers.

### Follow-up on confidentiality

> Quick check — is anything you're sharing confidential or under NDA? If yes, redact identifiers (customer names, dollar amounts) before pasting. The skill doesn't need them to draft a strong outline.

---

## Compressing intake

If the user has already answered some questions in their initial prompt, acknowledge what you have, then ask only what's missing. Example:

> You've already given me audience (SVP + chief of staff), time (20 min), and purpose (Q3 funding approval). Three things I still need before I can structure this:
>
> - **Technical depth**: how technical does she go in this forum?
> - **Desired outcome**: approval to *start* in Q3, or approval to *deliver* by end of Q3? Different ask.
> - **Existing material**: any prior deck on this topic, or a competing proposal in her queue I should position against?

---

## When the user pushes back on intake

If the user says "just draft something":

> I get the urge, but if I draft from assumptions I'll waste both our time. Three minutes of intake saves an hour of revision. Pick the three questions above that matter most and answer those — I can run with that.

If the user is on a tight deadline:

> Tight deadline noted. I'll cut intake to the two most-load-bearing questions: **audience** and **desired outcome**. Answer those and I'll draft. We can refine on a second pass.
